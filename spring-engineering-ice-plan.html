Spring Engineering & Manufacturing
ICE Plan

A Scanlon-based gainsharing and participation plan adopted in 1994, built on the "triangle" of Investors, Customers, and Employees — ensuring a fair and balanced return for all stakeholders.

✦
Scanlon / Frost Plan · Manufacturing · 1994

Spring Engineering & Manufacturing Corporation, a precision springs and stampings manufacturer founded in a Detroit basement in 1950, developed the ICE Plan — standing for Investors, Customers, and Employees — as an evolution of their existing Scanlon plan. Designed by an elected Ad-Hoc Committee, it addresses the shortcomings of their original formula by adding multi-indicator measurement, clearer participation structures, and a commitment to shared accountability. The plan was put to an employee vote in late 1994, requiring 85% approval to take effect for a 24-month trial period beginning January 1, 1995.

The Three Chapters

I

Chapter One

Identity

"We must understand that success for any of us means success for all of us."

The Identity chapter grounds every employee in Spring Engineering's history, current competitive position, and vision for becoming a World Class supplier. An informed workforce is the foundation of the ICE Triangle.

  • Company history from 1950 Detroit basement startup to 70+ employees
  • Major customers: Ford Motor, General Motors, Robert Bosch, Britax
  • Three stakeholder perspectives: Investor, Customer, Employee
  • Why change is needed — survival in a competitive industry
  • ICE Plan overview: participation + equity working together

Company History

Byron Tindall launched the business after-hours in his northwest Detroit basement around 1950. By 1952, he partnered with John Woods to officially form Spring Engineering & Manufacturing Corporation, starting in a 10x20 room on 8 Mile Road with less than $5,000. The company grew steadily, eventually moving to a 40,000 sq. ft. facility in 1979. After Byron's son Ken became president, his sudden passing in 1988 led Tim Tindall to take the helm — continuing the family tradition of steady growth.

The Stakeholder Triangle

Spring Engineering frames its business around three equally important stakeholders: the Investor (who provides capital and vision), the Customer (who demands quality, service, and on-time delivery), and the Employee (who wants job security, fair wages, recognition, and a voice). Each depends on the others. If any one side of the triangle weakens, the whole company is at risk.

Why the ICE Plan Was Needed

The existing Scanlon plan had shortcomings: it was too heavily weighted toward labor productivity, didn't measure scrap or on-time delivery, and didn't effectively motivate broad behavior change. The ICE Plan was built to fix these gaps — replacing a single-metric formula with five balanced performance indicators that employees can actually influence.

P

Chapter Two

Participation

"Participation is the value and responsibility is the process."

The Participation chapter builds a structured, plant-wide system for employees to submit ideas, review suggestions as teams, and drive measurable improvement — with clearly defined roles at every level of the organization.

  • Five-level structure: Employee → Dept. Teams → ICE Team → Management → President
  • Department teams meet weekly; suggestions reviewed by group consensus
  • ICE Coordinator logs, tracks, and routes every suggestion
  • Immediate implementation option (up to $1,500/quarter per department)
  • 4-week maximum response cycle; appeal process for declined ideas
Learn More ▾

The Suggestion Flow

An employee writes up an idea on a Suggestion Form and drops it in the department basket. The team leader brings it to the weekly department meeting, where the full team reviews it and votes by consensus — accepted or declined, with a written reason either way. Accepted suggestions may be implemented directly (within budget limits) or passed to the ICE Coordinator for further routing.

The ICE Team

The central ICE Team includes a representative from each department, plus quality, engineering, materials, accounting, the ICE Coordinator, and the president. Elected representatives serve six-month rotating terms, ensuring fresh perspectives. This team holds final review authority over suggestions, sets performance goals, approves quarterly bonus payments, and acts as custodian of the plan.

Suggestion Options

  • Immediate Implementation: Team can act on suggestions up to a $1,500 quarterly budget without ICE Team approval.
  • Forwarded to ICE Team: Suggestions affecting other departments or exceeding budget go to the full ICE Team for review.
  • Appeal: If a suggestion is declined, the suggester can present their case directly to the ICE Team.

Appropriate Suggestion Topics

Tools, safety, machine improvements, quality, customer service, and productivity are all in scope. Employment matters such as hiring, discipline, and personnel issues are handled through separate existing procedures — not the suggestion system.

E

Chapter Three

Equity

"A fair and balanced return to Investors, Customers, and Employees."

The Equity chapter defines how performance is measured and how financial gains are shared. Five indicators — each assigned a portion of the bonus pool — create clear, accountable targets with both upside rewards and downside consequences.

  • 5 indicators: Quality (12.5%), Scrap (12.5%), On-Time Delivery (12.5%), Operating Profit (12.5%), Productivity (50%)
  • Each indicator has a baseline, goal, and maximum opportunity
  • Negative performance reduces the bonus pool (downside consequences)
  • 25% of positive quarterly pools held in a reserve fund; 75% paid out
  • Distributed as % of qualifying wages (including overtime)
Learn More ▾

The Five Performance Indicators

  • Quality (12.5%): Parts Per Million (PPM) of external rejects — parts shipped to and rejected by customers.
  • Scrap (12.5%): PPM of internal rejects — parts caught before shipment that weren't sellable.
  • On-Time Delivery (12.5%): Average percentage of orders not late, measured daily against the promise date given to customers.
  • Operating Profit (12.5%): Net sales minus cost of goods sold and selling & administrative expenses. Baseline is the average operating profit over the prior three years.
  • Productivity (50%): Based on the original Scanlon ratio — controllable costs vs. Sales Value of Production. Controllable costs include direct/indirect labor, supervisory wages, overtime premium, healthcare, profit sharing, and office/engineering compensation.

How Bonuses Are Calculated

Each indicator has 21 possible levels of performance — 10 steps between baseline and goal, and an additional 10 steps between goal and maximum. Performing above baseline earns a positive payout; performing below baseline creates a negative consequence that reduces the pool. Each year, the ICE Team and management jointly set the dollar value per unit of improvement for each indicator.

Reserve Fund

In positive quarters, 25% of the bonus pool is set aside in a reserve fund; 75% is paid to employees. Negative quarters draw down the reserve. Any positive reserve balance at year-end is distributed; a negative balance is zeroed out. This protects the company and encourages employees to think long-term.

Eligibility

Employees must be on payroll on the date of payout and have 90 consecutive days of employment before the start of the new quarter to receive 100% of their allocation. Those completing their 90 days during the quarter receive credit for wages earned after that qualifying date.

This plan was developed in 1994 by an elected Ad-Hoc Committee at Spring Engineering & Manufacturing Corporation (Detroit, MI), a family-owned precision springs and stampings manufacturer founded in 1950. It was submitted for employee ratification in late 1994, requiring at least 85% approval to take effect as a 24-month trial beginning January 1, 1995. It represents a second-generation Scanlon plan that directly addressed the measurability and fairness shortcomings of the company's original Scanlon formula.
