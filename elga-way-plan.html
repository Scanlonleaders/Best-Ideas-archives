Principle I

Identity

ELGA Credit Union is a cooperative — a partnership between employees, members, management, board of directors, and the communities it serves. Founded January 31, 1951, to help underserved Consumers Energy employees in Genesee County, Michigan.

"Building member relationships through exceptional service while maintaining strong financial performance and supporting our communities."
1
We listen and interact to identify opportunities to provide genuine above and beyond service to ensure financial success for our members.
2
We go where we're afraid to go, do what we're afraid to do, and say what we're afraid to say — being courageous every single day.
3
We incorporate a fun atmosphere in our professional environment, making ELGA the greatest place to work, bank, and borrow.
4
We provide an environment for ongoing personal and professional growth, investing in every associate's development and potential.
1951
Founded January 31st to serve underpaid Consumers Energy employees. Name chosen via contest in 1959 combining "electrical" and "gas."
1970–80s
Introduced checking accounts, ATMs, and merged with three credit unions to counter shrinking membership. Risk-based lending made ELGA the only lender helping many members.
1990s–2000s
Overdraft protection, debit cards, XpressLink, e-Statements, online bill pay, grocery locations, and commercial lending launched. Membership grew to 20,326 by 2001.
2008–2011
Community charter expanded membership to six counties. 2008–2010 were the most challenging years. Assets reached $261.6M with 43,754 members by 2010.

Key Metrics & Goals

These quantitative measures define what success looks like for ELGA Credit Union stakeholders — guiding daily decisions and connecting every associate's actions to organizational health.

Efficiency Ratio
0.65
Cost to generate each $1 of revenue. The primary gain sharing trigger. Lower is better.
Member Service Survey
5.0
Scale of 1–5. Each point = 20% of payout eligibility. Score of 5 unlocks 100% of gain sharing.
Capital Ratio
≥ 9.5%
Regulatory reserve requirement. The "rainy day fund" ensuring viability through challenging times.
Return on Assets
≥ 1.5%
Net gain or loss divided by total assets. Measures profitability relative to the asset base deployed.
Delinquency Ratio
≤ 2%
Percentage of loan totals past due at least 60 days. Reflects underwriting quality and member financial health.
Loan to Share
≥ 90%
How much of member shares we've been able to loan out. Maximizing this ratio drives income for member benefits.
Services per Household
3.5 avg.
Must include a loan and/or VISA product. Multi-service households have stronger, longer-lasting relationships.
Community Involvement
Annual Goal
Hours associates give back to communities. Must be met for gain sharing to be paid. Goal set annually by management.
Board Duties
3 Duties
Duty of Care, Duty of Loyalty, and Duty of Obedience. Volunteer board governs strategy and fiduciary compliance.

Principle III

Equity

ELGA Credit Union recognizes three stakeholders: Members, Associates, and the Organization. All three must be treated equitably — creating a virtuous cycle of trust, performance, and shared gain.

Stakeholder 01
Equity to Members
  • Great service delivered reliably and quickly
  • Highest deposit rates and lowest loan rates
  • Low fees and transparent pricing
  • Corporate Social Responsibility and community support
Stakeholder 02
Equity to Associates
  • Fair compensation, benefits, and job security
  • Involvement and empowerment
  • Resources to do the job effectively
  • Share in the gains of the Credit Union
  • Clear and fair dispute resolution
Stakeholder 03
Equity to the Organization
  • Members contribute through products or fee income
  • Reserves built through profitability ensure viability
  • Relationship pricing creates sustainable membership tiers
  • Associates fulfill expectations to drive organizational health

Principle IV

Competency

Competency is the backbone of organizational improvement. The organization provides the tools — associates use them to continuously grow.

"The ability to respond to the constant demand for improvement and change."
Internal
CORE Employee Training
Four-level program ensuring individual competence in each job description — safely, efficiently, and skillfully performing every role.
Internal
Mentor Program
New-position associates paired with trained mentors. Ensures consistent skill levels across all branches, reducing variation and improving service.
Internal
Supervisor Development
Ongoing required courses and mentoring prepare employees for authority, preventing costly turnover from undertrained managers.
Online
CUES Online University
120+ courses in accounting, compliance, HR, lending, and management. One CUES module required per quarter. Accessible from any device.
Online
Software Skills (Lynda.com)
Introductory and advanced Microsoft Windows and Office training, enabling better use of business tools day-to-day.
External
Conferences and Seminars
Consultants, specialists, and organizations with field expertise. Used for job-specific competencies including conferences, seminars, and webinars.
Coaching
Individual Coaching Sessions
Associates work with managers to determine the right mix of job-specific and professional development learning for their career path.

Appendices A and B

Process Improvement Tools

Two new methodologies introduced with the Scanlon portion of The ELGA Way — both derived from LEAN thinking and used together or independently to drive systematic improvement.

Kaizen Blitz
Rapid improvement event — typically 3 to 8 days
1
Initial Analysis
Select Blitz area, compile team of up to 15 people. Develop a contract (empowerment statement) and mandate (aggressive yet achievable goal).
2
Kaizen Blitz Event
3 to 8 full days, 8 hours per day. Full training day, process analysis, baseline measurement, development and implementation of new processes, and formal presentation of accomplishments.
3
Follow Up
Team members visit affected areas for 5 to 10 days post-event to debug the process, complete action items, and ensure new processes are deployed across all applicable departments.
Plan-Do-Check-Act
Systematic change management cycle for sustained improvement
P
Plan
Define and Strategize
Define problem, validate with senior management, compile change control team, collect process data, flowchart, analyze, and generate solutions.
D
Do
Test at Small Scale
Develop testing plan and beta-implement the solution in a single, controlled branch environment before broader rollout.
C
Check
Evaluate Results
Review pilot data. Verify goals met. Discuss successes and failures. Validate the solution will positively impact all affected business units.
A
Act
Implement and Monitor
Standardize procedures, train all business units, install technology, and continuously monitor for compliance and sub-standard results.

Fair Process

Dispute Resolution

Employees who believe they have been treated unfairly may challenge any decision through ELGA's formal Problem Resolution System — without fear of discrimination or reprisal for using the system.

1
Step One
Supervisor Discussion
Employee discusses the concern directly with their supervisor. If the complaint concerns the supervisor, begin at Step 2.
2
Step Two
Manager and HR
Contact the manager supervisor (executive level management) and the HR department. Appeal must be in writing within 10 working days.
3
Step Three
CEO
If unresolved after Step 2, the employee pursues the matter directly with the CEO. The Credit Union will respond within 10 working days.
4
Step Four — Final
Board of Directors
If still unsatisfactory, employees may appeal to the Board of Directors. The Board's decision on the matter will be final.
