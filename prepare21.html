Introduction

Preparing Beth Israel
for the 21st Century

The economic and regulatory environments in which hospitals function are going through dramatic changes. Teaching hospitals, in particular, face growing challenges to their ability to provide quality patient care, excellent teaching, and leading-edge research.

In 1986, senior management began exploring participative management approaches. Inspired by the Scanlon Plan — a proven framework that fosters employee ownership of quality, productivity, and efficiency — a planning committee of 75 elected employees developed PREPARE/21.

The result is a living document — subject to renewal and revision to meet new needs of changing times. That very vitality and flexibility are among its greatest strengths.

Mission Statement · Board of Trustees, 1983
"The major mission of Beth Israel Hospital is to deliver patient care of the highest quality, in both scientific and human terms… carried out within a framework of financially responsible management which is sensitive to the requirements both to deliver health care which is efficient and cost-effective."
— Beth Israel Board of Trustees

Identity

Who We Are &
What We Stand For

Beth Israel employees, staff, and volunteers bring a diversity of ethnic, racial, and cultural backgrounds and a wealth of specialized training, skills, and experience. We are identical only in the quality of care we deliver and in the pride we take in doing so.

The hospital's mission is also that of its employees. When the hospital succeeds, each of us succeeds. Any failure diminishes us all; any success advances us all. To identify Beth Israel is to identify its people.

"The potential of Beth Israel Hospital is much more than the sum of our individual potentials. Our major challenge — and opportunity — is to find ways of tapping this energy."

— PREPARE/21 Plan

History

Beth Israel's
Turning Points

In the early 1900s, waves of immigrants arrived in Boston — most homeless, ill, and confused by cultural and language barriers. Boston's Jewish community took on the task of raising funds for a medical facility that respected the needs of this new population.

1901

Mount Sinai Hospital Association Founded

Incorporated to establish a Jewish-sponsored hospital open to all races, creeds, and colors.

1902

First Jewish Outpatient Clinic Opens

Boston's first Jewish outpatient facility opens in the old West End, serving the growing immigrant community.

1917

45-Bed Hospital Opens in Roxbury

The 45-bed facility is dedicated in October 1916 and opens for patients in February 1917, with a school of nursing enrolling 10 students.

1928

Harvard Medical School Affiliation

Beth Israel purchases property on Brookline Avenue near Harvard Medical School. The new 200-bed campus is dedicated in 1928, and the teaching affiliation with Harvard soon follows.

1970s

Major Modernization

The state-of-the-art Feldberg Building, Berenson Emergency Unit (Trauma Center), and primary nursing care model under Joyce C. Clifford transform the hospital into an internationally regarded academic medical center.

1988

PREPARE/21 Plan Approved

A planning committee of 75 elected employees approves the PREPARE/21 plan on 4 November 1988, marking the hospital's first employee-based participative management program.

✦ ✦ ✦

Beth Israel is also a pioneer in patient rights (first Statement on Rights of Patients, 1972), conservative breast cancer management, primary nursing care, insulin via nasal spray, early MRI cancer detection, and family-centered maternity care.

Participation

Work Teams &
Shared Governance

Participation is defined as the opportunity and the responsibility to influence the decision-making process within one's area of competence. It is both the right and the responsibility of every employee.

Role Responsibilities
President & Senior Mgmt Responsible and accountable for directing and leading the hospital through its Mission Statement and Mandate. Establishes goals for each division and service.
Departmental Work Team Every employee belongs to at least one. Meets 13 times per year (once each fiscal period). Primary source for receiving hospital performance information and generating ideas for improvement.
Functional Work Team Employees from several departments sharing a common work mission. Sets broader goals and reviews progress toward them across disciplines.
Ad Hoc Work Team Temporary task force convened to resolve a specific issue or evaluate a new idea. Disbands once the task is complete.
PREPARE/21 Council 20 councils (division and service) facilitate the review of ideas within divisions. Meets at least once per fiscal period; reports to the PREPARE/21 Coordinator.
Screening Committee Hospital-wide committee that reviews ideas affecting work teams outside an employee's own division or service. Chaired by a presidential appointee.
PREPARE/21 Coordinator Full-time position selected by the President and EVP. Coordinates day-to-day operations, training, and maintains records of all ideas submitted and implemented.

"The work team is the cornerstone of PREPARE/21. Every employee belongs to at least one. The success of PREPARE/21 will depend on work teams individually and collectively developing solutions to problems and generating ideas for improvement."

— PREPARE/21 Plan, Participation Section
Equity

Fair Return on
Collective Investment

"Equity" carries two meanings: that which is fair and just, and the sense of ownership we feel when we invest in something. At Beth Israel, patients, community, and employees all invest — and all deserve a return.

🏥

Patients' Equity

The highest quality medical care, teaching, and research delivered within a humanistic and ethical framework.

🏘️

Community's Equity

The hospital's continuing existence and constant improvement of resources to deliver the highest quality patient care.

👥

Employees' Equity

Enhanced job security, safer work conditions, professional growth, and a share in any monetary gains achieved through collective effort.

The Gain-Sharing Formula

Gains are measured at the overall hospital level each four-week fiscal period. Net gains are split 50/50 between the hospital and employees. An employee's share is calculated as a percentage of their period gross wages (capped at $50,000 annual base).

Source 01

Volume Factor

The difference between budgeted and actual patient admissions and outpatient visits, valued at a dollar amount approximating net revenue less variable costs.

Source 02

Savings Factor

The difference between budgeted and actual expenses (excluding depreciation, interest, and pension). Cost savings flow directly into the gain pool.

Source 03

Ideas Factor

The annualized dollar value of ideas accepted through the PREPARE/21 Idea Review Process, divided by 13 fiscal periods to calculate per-period savings.

A minimum gain of 0.5% must be exceeded for a payment to be made. 25% of each period's total gain is held in reserve, released at fiscal year end. Gains are paid on the last Thursday of the following period.

Idea Review Process

From Thought
to Action

The Idea Review Process gives every employee at Beth Israel the opportunity to contribute ideas that improve the hospital's overall performance. The best ideas identify ways to improve quality, reduce costs, increase revenue, or eliminate waste.

💡

Have an Idea

Consult the Idea Guide (Appendix D). Define what needs to change and why.

🤝

Discuss with Team

Bring the idea to your work team leader and affected colleagues for initial review.

📋

Submit Idea Form

Complete an Idea Form (Appendix E) and direct it to the appropriate Council or Screening Committee.

🔍

Review

Council or Screening Committee evaluates. Decision expected within 10 work days of meeting.

✅

Decision & Recognition

Written response sent to employee. If accepted, implementation begins and employee is publicly recognized.

What is NOT reviewed through this process

The process does not review personal complaints or problems such as difficulties in one's job, relationships with supervisors or peers, pay or benefits, or performance issues. These should be directed to the individual's manager or the Employee Relations Specialist.
